🛡️ Operation Sindoor (May 2026) — Why Kacholia's defence micro-caps just became 10x more relevant
India conducted precision military strikes against terrorist infrastructure in Pakistan in early May 2026. This was not just a military event — it is a fundamental shift in India's defence procurement priorities that will affect capital allocation for the next 5–10 years.

What changes for Indian defence companies post-Operation Sindoor:
1. Anti-drone / Counter-UAS systems: The conflict exposed India's vulnerability to drone-based threats. C-UAS (Counter-Unmanned Aircraft Systems) will receive emergency procurement funding — exactly what C2C Advanced Systems builds. The global C-UAS market was $6.64B in 2025, projected to hit $20.31B by 2030 at 25.1% CAGR. India's spending will accelerate significantly post this event.
2. Precision aerospace tooling: India is fast-tracking LCA Tejas Mk2, AMCA fighter programmes, and helicopter production. TechEra Engineering — which manufactures precision tooling (jigs, fixtures) for these aircraft — sits directly in this path. Every additional aircraft ordered = direct TechEra revenue.
3. Ammunition indigenisation: India's dependence on imported artillery shells was exposed. Balu Forge's 155mm shell line (already analyzed) is directly relevant.
4. C4ISR systems: Command, Control, Communications, Computers, Intelligence, Surveillance, Reconnaissance — the "digital backbone" of modern warfare. C2C Advanced Systems is India's emerging player here.

The thesis: Kacholia entered C2C Advanced and TechEra Engineering when they were tiny, unknown SME stocks. Post-Operation Sindoor, these are no longer obscure — they are strategically critical. But Kacholia is already in. The question for Garvit: is there still enough upside left?
Why micro-cap defence stocks can still 5–10x from here despite the rally
The tailwind argument — why they can still run
India's defence budget in FY27 is ₹7.41 lakh Cr. The "Atmanirbhar Bharat" defence indigenisation target means 75% of procurement must be from Indian vendors by FY28. For a company doing ₹100 Cr revenue — winning ONE government order of ₹50 Cr is a 50% revenue jump. These companies are so small that a single contract can re-rate them. Post-Sindoor, the urgency of indigenous procurement has increased dramatically. Small-cap defence stocks at ₹300–800 Cr mcap still have massive room to run if execution happens.
The risk argument — why they can also collapse
Defence micro-caps are the most dangerous type of investment. (1) Government procurement is notoriously slow and lumpy — a company can wait 3 years for a tender result. (2) Payment cycles are very long (180–365 day debtors). (3) Any quality/delivery issue = permanent contract loss. (4) At ₹300–500 Cr mcap with ₹30–100 Cr revenue, these stocks are technically illiquid. (5) Kacholia himself is reducing some positions (BEW Engineering, Radiowalla) — his exits can trigger 30–40% declines in illiquid stocks. Position size MUST be very small — treat as lottery tickets, not core positions.
