MFI sector context — India's microfinance industry and where Satin fits
🌱 India MFI sector — ₹4.3 lakh Cr market, serving 7.8 crore borrowers, structural growth story
India's NBFC-MFI sector provides collateral-free credit to women in rural and semi-urban areas — a segment with no access to formal banking. The sector serves 7.8 crore unique borrowers and has an outstanding portfolio of ₹3.9 lakh Cr (MFIs alone). RBI regulatory guardrails (Guardrail 1.0 and 2.0) cap borrower indebtedness: max 3 lenders per borrower, ₹2L loan cap for borrowers with income ≤₹3L.

Sector stress cycle (FY24–FY26) and where we are now: Over-leveraging of borrowers in FY23–24 caused a credit stress cycle in FY25–26. Collection efficiency dropped, NPA spiked. Satin outperformed with PAR 1 of 4.9% vs industry 16.9%. The stress appears to be bottoming: Q4 FY26 saw X-bucket CE recovering to 99.9% across most MFIs. RBI guardrails are reducing borrower leverage. The sector should normalize by FY27.

Satin's position in the sector: 3rd largest NBFC-MFI by AUM (₹15,275 Cr). Present in 29 states. 90,000+ villages. 2,015 branches. 33 lakh borrowers. Better PAR vs industry = better underwriting discipline.

Comprehensive risk assessment — be honest about what could go wrong
🔴 Top 4 risks — rank-ordered by severity
1. Promoter holding only 36.2% — the governance concern
Dr. HP Singh (CMD, founder) holds shares, but the overall promoter group at 36.2% is low compared to peers (Muthoot Microfin ~60%, CreditAccess Grameen ~70% via Grameen Koota). Low promoter holding means potential corporate governance dilution risk, block sales pressure, or management change risk. This is the single most important qualitative concern for Garvit's framework (prefers 55%+ promoter holding).

2. Credit cost normalization risk — what if it doesn't improve?
The entire 3x thesis depends on credit costs declining from 4% → 3% → 2.5% by FY28. If rural income stress continues (drought, political intervention, new state-level curbs), credit costs could stay elevated at 4%+. This would cap PAT at ₹200–230 Cr and the 3x math breaks down.

3. State-level political risk — similar to South India crisis
UP elections FY27, Rajasthan political dynamics. While historically less severe than South India, if any North India state introduces loan waivers or collection interference, Satin is disproportionately exposed given its 65% North India concentration. This is the mirror-image risk of what hit CreditAccess in South India.

4. RBI regulatory risk — further interest rate capping
RBI has been scrutinizing MFI lending rates and NIM. Any regulatory cap on lending rates (e.g., 24% to 20%) would compress NIM by 300-400 bps, directly hitting PAT by ₹80–100 Cr. This is a sector-wide risk affecting all MFIs equally, but Satin's current 14.25% NIM has some buffer.
