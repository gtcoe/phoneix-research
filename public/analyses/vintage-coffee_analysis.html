What Vintage Coffee does — the private label manufacturing model explained
The Private Label Model — supplying the world's coffee brands under their own name
Vintage Coffee does not have its own consumer brand. Instead, it manufactures instant coffee for other companies who sell it under their own brands. Think of it as the Foxconn of instant coffee — every major brand that sells instant coffee in Africa, the Middle East, Southeast Asia, and increasingly Europe, sources from manufacturers like Vintage. The manufacturer gets recurring volume orders without spending on consumer marketing. The brand owner gets consistent quality without building factories. This B2B model creates extremely sticky client relationships (98% retention) because switching supplier requires regulatory re-approval, quality re-certification, and taste profile re-matching. All of which take 6–12 months.
How the business works
1
Raw material sourcing: Robusta beans (80%) from Uganda, Vietnam, Indonesia + Arabica (20%) from India and others. 40% domestic / 60% import mix. Robusta is cheaper and suits instant coffee processing.
2
German turbo roaster + processing: Beans are roasted (proprietary hot-air roasting from German equipment), brewed, and then spray-dried to create soluble powder. Agglomerated version adds a granulation step for premium appearance.
3
Private label packaging: Products are packed in 25-kg bulk boxes (for commercial) OR in branded jars/sachets/pouches with the customer's own label. The customer brings their design; Vintage provides the coffee.
4
Export logistics: Ship from Hyderabad/Telangana via Kandla/Chennai/Nhava Sheva ports. Middle East and Africa are short-haul. Europe and US are longer routes where freeze-dried's value justifies the logistics cost.
Why this model is powerful — the compounding flywheel
+
Fixed cost leverage: Once a factory is built, each additional tonne of coffee processed has near-zero incremental fixed cost. EBITDA/kg rises from ₹114 (FY24) to ₹145 (FY25) purely because fixed costs are spread over more volume. At 16,000 MTPA, EBITDA/kg could reach ₹200+.
+
Order book visibility: Private label clients commit to annual purchase orders. The plant was fully booked for FY26 before FY26 started. This gives revenue visibility 12–18 months ahead — unlike most consumer companies.
+
No marketing spend: Vintage spends ₹0 on consumer advertising. All cost goes into manufacturing. This keeps SG&A lean and margins high vs FMCG companies.
→
Promoter pedigree: CMD Balakrishna Tati has 30+ years in international coffee sales. CEO C. Jawahar has 20+ years in coffee business. Founded with deep buyer relationships already established — they started getting orders before the factory was built.

Risk analysis — the full picture
1
Promoter holding only 34.6% — the lowest of any stock in your portfolio.
This is a critical governance concern for a small company. Institutional investors hold more than promoters. Balakrishna Tati and family have diluted heavily through preferential issues. Every equity raise further dilutes this. When promoters hold less than 40%, they have less skin-in-the-game and can be vulnerable to hostile actions or can exit further. Screener specifically flags: "Promoter holding is low: 34.6%." This is not a reason to not invest, but it is a non-negotiable monitoring point — if promoter holding falls below 30%, reduce position.
2
FDC plant execution risk — ₹450 Cr bet on a new product category.
Freeze-dried coffee manufacturing is technically more complex than spray-dried. The equipment is imported from Europe. Commissioning requires specialized technicians. Quality consistency in FDC is harder to achieve. Vintage has never made FDC before — this is a first-time product for the company. CCL Products, their peer, has been making FDC for years. A bad FDC batch can damage client relationships permanently. The first 6–12 months of FDC production will be technically high-risk.
3
Coffee bean (Robusta) price volatility is an uncontrollable input cost risk.
Robusta coffee prices surged 60–80% in 2024–25 due to droughts in Vietnam and Brazil. Since ~60% of raw material is imported, INR/USD also impacts input costs. The company manages this through fixed-price contracts and passing on cost to clients — but there is always a lag quarter where margins compress. In Q3 FY26, margins were partially impacted by commodity prices despite strong revenue growth.
4
Concentrated geography risk — Middle East and Africa = ~38% of revenue.
Any geopolitical issue in the Gulf region, currency devaluation in African markets, or trade policy change can impact this segment. Russia/CIS (18%) is another risk given ongoing sanctions environment. Vintage is actively diversifying into Europe and US but these are still small (10% + 4%).
5
Customer concentration — top few clients = significant revenue.
With 19+ clients and 98% retention, the diversification seems fine. But in private label manufacturing, the top 3–5 clients typically represent 60–70% of revenue. Losing one large client (who might build their own factory or switch to CCL Products) could significantly impact revenue in the short term.
+
Mitigants:
Order book fully pre-booked — 11,000 MTPA sold for FY26 before the plant came online. 98% client retention over multiple years — switching cost is high (regulatory re-approval, taste-match). OCF turned positive Q3 FY26 — self-sustaining business model emerging. Coffee export sector has strong government support (no export duty). German roasting equipment = quality moat vs cheaper domestic alternatives.

India's instant coffee sector — who plays where
The instant coffee sector has two distinct sub-businesses. (1) Private label B2B manufacturing (CCL Products, Vintage Coffee — export bulk/branded packs to global brands), and (2) Consumer FMCG brands (Nestle, Tata Coffee, HUL — sell directly to consumers). The economics are completely different. Private label gets volume certainty without marketing cost. FMCG gets brand premiums but bears advertising expense.

Different risk profiles — both valid
The Prestige analogy (from analyst report): CCL Products is the "incumbent" — delivers consistent, respectable returns. Vintage is the "challenger" — looks weak on ROE early, then snaps into high-return mode as operations mature. CCL's DNA is 30 years deep with professional management.
