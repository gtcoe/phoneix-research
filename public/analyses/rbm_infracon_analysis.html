🛢️ The ONGC Nandej Contract — the centrepiece of the entire RBM thesis
In September 2024, RBM Infracon won a 15-year Production Enhancement Contract from ONGC for the Nandej oil field in Gujarat. Contract value: ₹3,498 Cr (₹3,371 Cr crude + ₹127 Cr gas). RBM took over operations on January 17, 2025 and holds a 66% revenue share. The company has since drilled 2 wells (NJ134 and NJ135) with a combined peak potential of 100 barrels per day (BOPD) from April 2026. This single contract is worth 7.8x RBM's entire market cap and is the primary reason the order book exploded from essentially nothing to ₹4,531 Cr. Every metric in the analysis must be understood in this context.

Risks — this is a higher-risk stock than it appears on the headline numbers
1
Extreme client concentration — ONGC is 77% of order book.
The ₹4,531 Cr order book sounds massive. But ₹3,498 Cr of it is one contract — ONGC Nandej. If ONGC terminates, disputes the contract, or if the oil field underperforms, RBM's revenue drops to ~₹150–200 Cr instantly. Every egg is in the ONGC basket. Compare this to DEE Development, which has a diversified order book across 50+ clients in oil/gas, nuclear, and water sectors. Single-client dependency is the most significant structural risk for RBM.
2
Crude oil price risk — ONGC revenue is market-linked.
The Nandej contract generates revenue at market-linked crude prices. At $80/barrel = strong margins. At $55/barrel = compressed margins. At $40/barrel (a scenario we've seen) = potential loss-making in that segment. RBM has no hedging mechanism for crude prices. This introduces commodity price risk to what looks like a construction/EPC company — an unusual and often unappreciated risk factor. With India's oil import dependence, a global crude crash also hits refinery client capex (reducing EPC orders) simultaneously. Double hit scenario is possible.
3
SME-listed company with very low liquidity — ₹7,000 shares/day trading volume.
RBM Infracon raised only ₹8.37 Cr in its 2022 IPO on the SME platform. It has not yet migrated to the main board (planned but not done). Trading volume is ~7,000 shares/day — at ₹422/share, that's ₹29.5 lakh of daily liquidity. Garvit holds 400 shares worth ~₹1.69 lakh. In a falling market, selling 400 shares could itself move the price 5–10%. This illiquidity means: (a) pricing is volatile, (b) institutional investors cannot enter, (c) exit in a panic is painful. The 52-week range of ₹267–₹525 (nearly 2x swing) reflects this volatility.
4
April 2026 diversification sprint — classic "kitchen sink" pattern.
In one week (April 16–22, 2026): MoA amended to add aerospace/drones, Oman LLC incorporated, $1B semiconductor LOI signed, oil well production announced. This is either a brilliantly opportunistic company seizing multiple opportunities — or a small company flinging announcements to maintain stock momentum. The semiconductor LOI is the most concerning: SUJOK Global is unverified, the technology (advanced chip packaging) is completely unrelated to RBM's core EPC business, and the LOI is subject to 90-day due diligence. These announcements drove the 17% single-day surge — but whether any materialise into real business by FY27 is a separate question.
5
GST demand of ₹9.94 Cr — auditor-noted liability.
The GST demand (₹9.94 Cr, with ₹4.30 Cr already paid) appeared in the April 2026 disclosure as an auditor note. For a company with PAT of ~₹46 Cr, a ₹9.94 Cr demand is material (~22% of annual profit). The full demand could be contested but represents a genuine liability. It also raises questions about tax compliance quality — something that wouldn't typically surface in auditor notes for well-governed companies.
6
Oil field operations = new competency, not proven track record.
Operating an oil field (drilling, extraction, production management) is fundamentally different from EPC construction. RBM has 25+ years of EPC experience but zero prior oil production experience before the Nandej contract. The fact that they drilled 2 successful wells within 3 months is encouraging — but long-term oil field management (well workovers, enhanced recovery, reservoir management) requires expertise that takes years to build. Any well integrity failure or production disappointment at Nandej directly impacts the core investment thesis.
