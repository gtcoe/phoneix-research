Websol's story is one of the boldest technology pivots in Indian manufacturing. Management scrapped 100% of existing 250 MW capacity in FY23 (deliberately zero-ing revenue) to upgrade to Mono PERC — a decision that required enormous conviction and caused 2 loss-making years. The reward: a business that emerged with 44% EBITDA margins and 35% PAT margins — significantly higher than its former self, and extraordinarily high for any Indian manufacturer. This discipline is the most important qualitative signal about Websol's management.

The ALMM List-II — why it's a game-changing regulatory moat
From June 2026, ALL government solar projects must use cells from ALMM List-II approved manufacturers. Currently, only ~8 Indian manufacturers are approved. Websol is one of them. This creates a structural demand floor — every government, CPSU, PM-KUSUM, PM Surya Ghar project in India (projected at ₹37 GW/year from FY26–FY30) must source cells from List-II manufacturers or face project cancellation. China-manufactured cells are explicitly excluded. This is regulatory protection at its most powerful — the government has effectively mandated that Websol's customers have no alternative to buying from them for key projects.

The DCR premium is the key: Websol sells cells to Indian module manufacturers who need them to comply with Domestic Content Requirements. These cells command a 30–50% price premium over Chinese-imported cells because they're essential for accessing government projects. As long as DCR enforcement continues (and it's getting stricter), this premium persists.
What Websol does — pure-play solar cell manufacturer in a regulated market
Websol is NOT a solar EPC company (it doesn't install solar panels). It manufactures the solar cells that go inside solar modules, which then go into solar power plants. This is an upstream manufacturing play — more like a semiconductor fab than a construction company. The key distinction: Websol benefits from rising solar demand even if EPC companies are struggling, because more solar projects = more demand for their cells.
The business in detail
1
What they make: Mono PERC (currently) and TOPCon (from FY27) solar photovoltaic cells and modules. A cell is the fundamental unit of a solar panel — multiple cells form a module. Websol makes both but cells are the higher-value, higher-margin product.
2
Revenue model: Sell cells to module manufacturers (Rayzon, Goldi, Luminous, Exide, Panasonic) who need Indian-made cells to meet DCR requirements. The module manufacturers then supply to solar project developers. Websol = B2B upstream manufacturer, not consumer-facing.
3
Location advantage — Falta SEZ, West Bengal: Special Economic Zone status provides customs duty exemptions on imported raw materials (silicon wafers, silver paste, anti-reflection coatings). Lower input costs vs mainland-based manufacturers.
!
The raw material dependency: Websol currently imports silicon wafers from China (primary feedstock). The MoU with Linton aims to eventually manufacture ingots and wafers in India — backward integration that would further reduce costs and risks.
The technology leap — PERC to TOPCon
→
Mono PERC (current): Passivated Emitter and Rear Cell technology. Websol's efficiency: 23.2–23.6%. Market standard. All current 1.2 GW runs on PERC.
+
TOPCon (Phase III & IV): Tunnel Oxide Passivated Contact. Higher efficiency (24–25%+). Lower degradation per year. Commands 5–10% price premium over PERC. Global shift happening now — TOPCon is the dominant new technology. The 4 GW AP expansion will be entirely TOPCon.
+
G12R transition (Falta, FY27): Larger wafer format (G12R = 210×105mm). Required by module manufacturers for higher-wattage modules that are now standard in utility projects. One existing Falta line being ramped to G12R by mid-FY27.
+
Backward integration — Ingot/Wafer (MoU with Linton): Currently Websol imports wafers from China. Linton (global leader in ingot/wafer) MoU explores making wafers domestically. If successful: ~₹5/Wp saving on raw material + DCR compliance from wafer level.

Client stickiness is high in DCR solar cells. Module manufacturers who use Websol's cells have built their production lines around Websol's cell format and efficiency specifications. Switching supplier requires re-qualifying cells (3–6 months), re-calibrating lines, and risking project delays. This is not a spot commodity market — it's a relationship business.
Management guidance — historical track record & future commitments
Context is critical for Websol's guidance history. The company has been in a transformation phase — scrapping old technology, building new, and now scaling aggressively. Some guidance misses were due to deliberate strategy (delaying production to get the technology right), not execution failure. Evaluating guidance credibility requires separating technology-related delays from operational misses.
Past guidance vs actual — the transformation era (FY23–FY25)
✗ FY23 Guidance: "Minimal revenue disruption during transition"
Actual: Revenue fell from ₹213 Cr to ₹17 Cr (-92%). The decision to completely dismantle the old 250 MW line caused a near-zero revenue year. However, this was strategically correct — running the old inferior technology would have produced low-margin output that competed poorly. The "miss" was a necessary sacrifice.
Credibility verdict: Not a real miss — management was honest about the severity of the pivot in hindsight. Strategic call was right.
⏱ FY24 Guidance: 600 MW PERC Phase I commissioned by H1 FY24
Actual: Commissioned by Jan 2024 (minor delay vs initial June 2023 target). The delay was primarily because management chose to self-fund (internal accruals) after getting IREDA ₹179 Cr loan rather than rush. Phase I delivered 90% utilisation once stabilised — demonstrating excellent operational execution post-commission.
Credibility verdict: 6-month delay, but execution quality once live was exceptional.
✓ FY25 Guidance: ₹500+ Cr revenue, margins recover significantly
Actual: Revenue ₹575 Cr (+15% beat), EBITDA 33.6%, PAT ₹155 Cr (massive beat). The Phase I line hit 90% utilisation quickly, margins exceeded expectations because DCR demand was stronger than projected. This was the year the market re-rated Websol from "turnaround story" to "high-quality manufacturer."
Credibility verdict: Strong beat. Management underguided revenue by ~15%.
✓ Q3 FY26: Phase II commissioned in under 12 months, self-funded
Phase II (600 MW) was guided for Oct 2025. Actual: commissioned Sep 27, 2025 — one week EARLY. Funded entirely from internal accruals (no equity dilution). Achieved 23.6% peak cell efficiency within 3 months of commissioning (very fast ramp). Line 1 maintained 97% utilisation while Line 2 ramped. This is excellent execution credibility.
Credibility verdict: Beat on timing, beat on cost (self-funded), beat on efficiency. Best possible outcome.
Forward guidance — what management is committing to
🎯 Phase III — 2 GW TOPCon at Andhra Pradesh by June 2027
70:30 debt-equity mix. ₹1,500 Cr capex. Land allotted at MPSEZ Naidupeta. Government incentives: capital investment subsidy, land benefits, duty exemptions. Captive 100 MW solar plant planned. Technology: TOPCon (next-gen, 24–25% efficiency). G12R format. If commissioned by June 2027, FY28 revenue nearly doubles from FY27. This is the most critical guidance item to track.
Credibility: Medium-High (7/10). Land secured, incentives granted, Websol has proven Phase II execution. But 2 GW greenfield in a new state is 3x the scale of anything done before. June 2027 is aggressive — H2 FY28 is the more likely commissioning.
🎯 Phase IV — 2 GW TOPCon at Andhra Pradesh by June 2028
Builds on Phase III at same site. ₹1,500 Cr additional capex. By FY28 completion, Websol will have 5.2 GW cell and 4.5 GW module capacity — placing it among India's top 3 solar manufacturers. Total capex across phases III+IV = ₹3,000+ Cr, funded 70% debt / 30% equity.
Credibility: Medium (6/10). Phase IV is contingent on Phase III success. June 2028 target is more achievable but still depends on debt financing, TOPCon technology stabilisation, and continued DCR demand.
🎯 G12R transition at Falta — one line by mid-FY27
One existing Falta PERC line being upgraded to G12R format (larger wafer = higher wattage modules) by mid-FY27. No new capex required — this is a reconfiguration. G12R commands 5–8% price premium and is increasingly required by large module manufacturers for utility-scale projects.
Credibility: High (8/10). Low-risk upgrade. Websol has done this kind of reconfiguration before (they rebuilt their entire shopfloor in FY23). No new capital required.
🎯 Backward integration — Linton MoU (ingot/wafer manufacturing)
Signed MoU with Linton (global PV ingot/wafer leader) to explore domestic wafer manufacturing in India. If executed, Websol eliminates its primary raw material import dependency from China (wafers = ~60–70% of cell manufacturing cost). This would also qualify Websol for even stricter DCR compliance from the wafer level, further strengthening its regulatory moat.
Credibility: Low-Medium (4/10). MoU ≠ execution. Ingot/wafer manufacturing requires entirely new technology and capex. This is 3–5 year option value, not near-term catalyst. Timeline undefined.

Risks — the full picture
1
₹3,000 Cr capex is 94% of current market cap — massive execution risk.
Websol's total market cap is ~₹3,200 Cr. The Phase III + IV expansion costs ₹3,000+ Cr. Even at 70:30 debt-equity, the equity requirement (~₹900 Cr) could trigger significant dilution. Long-term debt will rise from current 0.29x D/E to potentially 3–4x D/E during the expansion. If the new plants are delayed by 12–18 months, the company will be servicing large debt without commensurate revenue — a cash flow stress scenario.
2
Chinese solar cell competition is severe and structural.
Chinese TOPCon cells currently cost ~$0.042/Wp (₹3.57/Wp) vs Websol's DCR-premium pricing at an estimated ₹7–10/Wp. That's a 2–2.5x price gap. Websol can only maintain its premium as long as DCR/ALMM regulations force Indian module manufacturers to source domestically. If regulations are relaxed (possible under WTO pressure), Websol's premium evaporates. The government has strengthened DCR enforcement in recent years, but this is always a policy risk.
3
EBITDA margin compression as scale increases is almost certain.
At 1.2 GW, Websol's 44% EBITDA margin is extraordinary because: (a) DCR premium pricing, (b) low overhead on a small revenue base, (c) Falta SEZ duty exemptions. At 5.2 GW (2028E), the TOPCon lines at AP will have: (a) higher capex depreciation, (b) higher interest costs, (c) potentially tighter DCR pricing as more Indian manufacturers compete. Management acknowledges 25–30% EBITDA margins at full scale (vs current 44%). Revenue will be 5–6x higher, but PAT margin will compress — overall PAT can still be 3–4x higher than today in absolute terms.
4
Silver price volatility — the silent input cost risk.
Silver is the primary metal in solar cell manufacturing (used for conductor paste). Silver prices have been elevated globally. Websol's profitability is directly sensitive to silver prices — a 20% increase in silver cost could reduce EBITDA margins by 3–5 percentage points. Management mentioned this as a key cost risk in Q3 FY26 earnings call. Hedging capabilities for a company this size are limited.
5
US tariff risk — indirect but real.
Management clarified Websol has no US exports currently, so the 2025 Trump tariffs don't directly affect them. But: Indian solar exports to the US account for ~40% of Waaree's revenue. If US tariffs reduce Waaree's US business, Waaree may redirect capacity toward domestic India market, increasing competition for DCR cells. Indirectly, any disruption in India's solar export market could increase domestic competition and compress Websol's DCR premium.
+
Key mitigants:
ALMM List-II approval = regulatory protected demand. D/E 0.29x = significant debt headroom for the ₹3,000 Cr expansion without immediate distress. Self-funded Phase II demonstrates execution discipline. AP government incentives (capital subsidy, duty exemptions, land) reduce Phase III/IV effective cost. 30-year track record in solar manufacturing = technology credibility that new entrants lack. 37 GW/year solar addition required FY26–FY30 = enormous sustained demand.

Oriana Power — deeper context (why it's different from Websol)
Oriana's strengths vs Websol
+
Diversified revenue model: EPC (one-time project revenue) + RESCO/BOOT (15–25 year annuity contracts). Websol has only manufacturing revenue. Oriana's RESCO/annuity component creates durable recurring income Websol lacks.
+
BESS first-mover: 403 MWh BESS orders in FY25. Target 1 GWh FY26, 3.5 GWh by FY30. BESS is structurally the next massive value creator in India's energy sector — Oriana has early positioning.
+
FY26 revenue guidance ₹2,500–2,600 Cr: On ₹4,308 Cr mcap, this is a P/S of ~1.7x on forward revenue — very cheap. FY25 PAT ₹232 Cr (TTM), 18–19x PE is reasonable.
+
Green molecules optionality: ₹3,135 Cr green ammonia deal with SECI, e-methanol pilot. If green hydrogen scales, Oriana has early positioning that Websol completely lacks.
Oriana's risks vs Websol
!
Debtor days: 107 → 146 days — rising sharply. EPC companies collect cash when projects are certified complete. Delays in project completion = cash gets locked up. At ₹1,409 Cr revenue, 146-day debtors = ₹560+ Cr in receivables. This is a real cash flow stress risk that Websol (manufacturing B2B) doesn't have.
!
EPC is not a recurring business: Each project must be won freshly. Order book of ₹3,000 Cr sounds large but is ~2x revenue — less visibility than Websol's DCR-protected demand floor. If order wins slow down, revenue growth stalls instantly.
!
Execution complexity across too many segments: Solar EPC, BESS, green ammonia, e-methanol, biogas, floating solar — Oriana is running 6–7 business lines simultaneously. Management bandwidth is a real concern at ₹4,308 Cr market cap.
!
Green H2 / ammonia is pre-revenue: The ₹3,135 Cr green ammonia deal is an offtake agreement — not current revenue. Building green ammonia infrastructure requires years and billions. At current stage, this is option value, not near-term earnings.

The definitive verdict for Garvit's 3x goal
Websol Energy — Verdict: High conviction buy. ₹40,000–60,000. Best solar manufacturing play for 3x.
Websol is the best-positioned solar manufacturing stock for an aggressive 3x goal in the Indian listed universe. Here's why it wins over Waaree and Premier:

1. Margins: 44% EBITDA margin vs 14–25% for peers — Websol extracts nearly 3x more EBITDA per rupee of revenue. This is because cell manufacturing (Websol's core) has higher value-add than module assembly (Waaree's core).

2. Size advantage for 3x math: At ₹3,200 Cr mcap, 3x = ₹9,600 Cr. Achievable if Phase III delivers and PAT reaches ₹600–700 Cr. At Waaree's ₹52,000 Cr, 3x = ₹1.56 lakh Cr — practically impossible.

3. Clean balance sheet entering a capital-intensive phase: D/E 0.29x means Websol can absorb ₹2,000–2,500 Cr of new debt for Phase III/IV without financial distress. Contrast with competitors who already carry heavy debt.

4. Regulatory moat: ALMM List-II approval. Zero US export exposure. Entirely India-focused DCR business — no tariff risk.

The risk: ₹3,000 Cr capex is a big bet for a ₹3,200 Cr company. Phase III delays of 12–18 months would defer the 3x outcome by 1–2 years but not break the thesis. The 1.2 GW Falta business alone can deliver 2x in 2 years.
Premier Energies — Better for 2x, not 3x (₹0 for Garvit's aggressive goal)
Premier is the better-quality, more integrated, better-orderbooked company at ₹13,700 Cr order book. But at ₹15,000+ Cr mcap, 3x needs ₹45,000 Cr — very ambitious. Better as a 2x play in a more conservative portfolio. JM Financial's "reduce" call at current levels reflects expensive valuation (35–40x PE) relative to earnings delivery.
Waaree Energies — Best company, worst 3x math (₹0 for this goal)
Waaree is India's largest solar manufacturer with dominant scale. But at ₹52,000+ Cr mcap, 3x = ₹1.56 lakh Cr. And US tariff risk on 40% of revenue is a real near-term headwind. EBITDA margin at 14–17% is the lowest in the sector. Own Waaree for 5+ year compounding, not 3x in 3 years.
