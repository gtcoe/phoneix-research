Why EBITDA margins are thin — and whether they will expand
The margin debate is the single most important analytical question for Zaggle. EBITDA at 9% looks terrible for a company positioning itself as a SaaS business. The reason: cashback expenses rose 70% YoY in Q2 FY26 to ₹40 Cr, H1 cashback at ₹75 Cr (+63.9% YoY). Cashback is the cost of acquiring/retaining prepaid card users — similar to customer acquisition cost (CAC) in SaaS but booked as an operating expense. As the installed base matures, cashback as % of revenue should decline (existing users don't need as much incentive). Management guided margin expansion toward 10–11% by FY27. If this materialises, PAT margins reach 9–10%, translating to ₹200+ Cr PAT on ₹2,300 Cr revenue.

Products — the full Zaggle ecosystem
Zaggle's genius is occupying the intersection of SaaS and fintech. Most SaaS companies have no financial product. Most fintech companies have no SaaS layer. Zaggle combines both — a software platform (that companies pay for) embedded with financial products (prepaid cards, expense cards). This "SaaS + float" model creates switching costs (deep workflow integration) AND a recurring fintech revenue stream (interchange, float). Global analogue: Brex, Ramp, Expensify in the US — but targeting India's far less penetrated SME/enterprise market.

The B2B2C flywheel — why it creates network effects
1
Enterprise signs up (B2B layer): A company like Blue Star signs a 3-year Zaggle Save contract. Zaggle integrates with their HRMS and payroll. Integration takes 2–4 weeks — after that, switching cost is very high (data history, workflow dependency, employee habits).
2
Employees receive prepaid cards (B2C layer): All Blue Star employees now use Zaggle prepaid cards for meal/fuel/telecom reimbursements. Each employee becomes a Zaggle card user — a consumer touchpoint that generates interchange every time they swipe.
3
Data flywheel: With 3.7M users and 50M+ cards, Zaggle has unprecedented India-specific corporate spending data. This powers ZatiX analytics — which in turn sells back to enterprises as "spend intelligence." Data → product → sales → more data. A classic platform flywheel.
+
Why this beats pure prepaid card companies: HDFC Bank or Axis Bank could issue prepaid cards. But they can't build a Propel rewards platform, a Save tax benefit workflow, or a Zoyer expense reporting system — these are SaaS products requiring engineering depth, not banking licenses. Zaggle's moat is the SaaS layer that banks can't easily replicate.

B2B fintech spend management — India and global landscape
India's corporate expense management and employee benefits market is massively underpenetrated. Analyst estimates: India spends ₹40–50 lakh crore annually on employee salaries and benefits. Of this, formal digital expense management adoption is less than 5%. Zaggle's current ₹1,700 Cr revenue barely scratches the surface. The structural tailwind is real — the question is who wins the lion's share as the market formalises.

Zaggle's SaaS software layer is its differentiation that these players don't yet match.
