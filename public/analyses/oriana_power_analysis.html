The fundamental question: Was the satellite ₹20K recommendation correct?
The debtor days concern we flagged has actually worsened. At the time of our original analysis, we noted "debtor days 146 rising" as a warning. Screener now shows debtor days have increased from 107 (FY24) to 146 days (TTM). Working capital days went from 32.8 to 81.5 — a 148% deterioration. In a solar EPC business with aggressive revenue growth, rising debtor days typically mean: (a) government/utility clients are slow-paying, (b) the company is booking revenue before cash is collected, or (c) project disputes are emerging. None of these is benign for a ₹4,232 Cr mcap company with 22.9% paper gains sitting in a portfolio.
But the business has also become more interesting since we first analyzed it. Oriana Power is not just a solar EPC company anymore. It has secured 403 MWh of BESS orders (targets 1 GWh FY26, 3.5 GWh FY30), signed a ₹3,135 Cr green ammonia GAPA with SECI (April 2026), secured a ₹1,180 Cr floating solar EPC order (234 MW at DVC Maithon), and is entering data centre power supply. The business has become more complex, more ambitious, and potentially much more valuable — but also carries higher execution risk.

Risks — the full honest picture on a fast-growing multi-vertical company
🚨 The Debtor Days Problem — 107 → 146 days: This is the core risk
Debtor days rising from 107 to 146 days (+36%) means Oriana is completing solar projects and booking revenue, but customers are paying later and later. In solar EPC, this typically means: (a) government utility clients with bureaucratic payment processes (RVUNL, DISCOMS), (b) disputed project milestones, or (c) aggressive revenue recognition. For a company growing revenue from ₹987 Cr to ₹2,250 Cr, if debtor days stay at 146, accounts receivable would reach ₹900+ Cr — nearly equal to one full year's PAT over the next 3 years. If even 15–20% of these receivables are disputed/uncollectable, it wipes out a full year of profit. Working capital days went from 32.8 to 81.5 — another major warning. This single metric is the primary reason we are NOT recommending a full conviction position in Oriana despite the spectacular growth.
1
SME board listing — limited institutional oversight and liquidity constraints.
Oriana Power is still listed on the NSE SME platform (Emerge board), not the main board. This means: lower disclosure requirements than main board, limited institutional research coverage, and liquidity risks for larger positions. At ₹4,232 Cr market cap, Oriana is well above the typical SME size — main board migration should be imminent, but until it happens, governance and transparency are less rigorously enforced than main board peers like Websol, DEE Development, or Borana Weaves. Garvit's 75-share position at ₹1.52L is large relative to typical SME daily volumes.
2
BESS execution is new and untested at scale — 3.5 GWh by FY30 is ambitious.
Oriana entered BESS in FY25. The BOO model for BESS requires: sourcing battery cells (mostly imported from China/Korea), managing grid connectivity (separate clearance from EPC), and delivering 12–25 year operating track record that utilities require. The 403 MWh secured in FY25 is impressive, but execution is the proof. BOO BESS means Oriana carries balance sheet risk (owns the asset, collects revenue) — not just EPC risk. If BESS projects face technology or grid issues, they sit on Oriana's books as non-performing assets.
3
Green hydrogen / ammonia — gigafactory risk is very real.
Green hydrogen is still a commercially pre-viable technology at most scales. The SECI GAPA (₹52.25/kg for green ammonia) is at the top end of current electrolyser cost-effectiveness. If electrolyser costs don't decline as projected, the gigafactory becomes economically unviable at ₹52.25/kg. The MoU for gigafactory land in Madhya Pradesh is a very early step — this project is 3–5 years from commercial production. Until then, it's option value, not revenue.
4
Client concentration in BESS — government utilities slow payment.
BESS BOO clients (RVUNL, TGGENCO, KPTCL) are state-owned utilities — the same category of client responsible for rising debtor days in solar EPC. If BESS BOO revenue follows the same slow-payment pattern as EPC, the working capital situation worsens further. The BOO model (build, own, operate) means Oriana takes on execution risk AND offtake risk simultaneously.
