Understanding the PAT vs EBITDA disconnect: Awfis has 36.5% EBITDA margins but only 4–6% PAT margins. The gap is explained by Ind AS 116 accounting — all lease payments (rent to building owners) are booked as depreciation + interest, not as operating expense. This inflates EBITDA but reduces PAT. The correct business metric to use is Adjusted EBITDA (post-lease-costs) or EBITDA minus lease payments, not standard EBITDA. At the operating level, Awfis is genuinely improving — but the PAT numbers are structurally depressed by the lease accounting standard that all co-working companies globally face.

Why occupancy expansion is the most powerful margin lever
The operating leverage of co-working is extraordinary: A center with 500 seats has fixed costs of ₹15–20L/month (rent in MA model is waived; for owned leases, it's depreciation). At 70% occupancy (350 seats), it may break even. At 85% occupancy (425 seats), incremental revenue of 75 seats at ₹8–10K/seat/month = ₹6–7.5L/month additional revenue at near-100% margin (no additional cost). Each 1% improvement in blended occupancy across 152,000 seats = ~1,520 more seats × ₹9,000 avg = ₹13.7 Cr additional annual revenue — almost entirely falling to EBITDA/PAT.

How Awfis makes money — three business segments
Awfis is NOT a simple co-working company. It operates three distinct businesses: (1) Co-working and Allied Services (core, high-margin recurring), (2) Design & Build / Awfis Transform (construction/fit-out, being separated), and (3) Managed Aggregation (capital-light white-label co-working for building owners). Understanding all three is essential.
🏢 Co-working & Allied Services (84% of Q3 FY26 revenue)
Core business · Growing 32% YoY · High recurring revenue
Awfis rents office space, fits it out, and sub-leases as flexible workspaces. Revenue: monthly seat fee from clients (enterprise, SME, individual). Two models: (a) Lease model — Awfis takes direct lease from building owner, owns the client relationship fully. Higher capex, higher margin. (b) MA model — building owner brings Awfis to manage their space; Awfis earns a management fee. Capital-light, ~67% of portfolio. Allied services: facility management, IT support, event hosting, mobility solutions (ECOS partnership for employee transport).

The MA vs Lease model split — why 67% MA is a strength
Managed Aggregation (67% of portfolio)
+
Building owner funds the capex — Awfis brings clients and management expertise. Awfis earns a revenue share (30–40% of seat revenue) without any capital investment. This is the WeWork global failure's lesson learned — WeWork over-leveraged on direct leases. Awfis uses MA to grow without balance sheet risk.
+
Risk: if occupancy falls below breakeven, building owner bears the loss, not Awfis. In a downturn, the MA model protects Awfis's balance sheet. This is why Awfis survived where WeWork globally collapsed — the risk architecture is fundamentally different.
Lease model (33% of portfolio)
+
Higher margin when occupancy is good: Awfis keeps all revenue above the fixed lease cost. At 85%+ occupancy, lease model centers are 35–40% EBITDA margin vs 23–27% for MA centers. These are Awfis's most profitable assets.
!
Risk if occupancy falls: Lease model centers have fixed rent even if seats are empty. Under Ind AS 116, this shows as depreciation + interest — depressing PAT even if occupancy temporarily dips. This is the lease accounting distortion that makes PAT look worse than the underlying business.

Risks — what keeps this from being a higher-conviction pick
🚨 Promoter holding at 17% — the biggest structural concern
Awfis was founded by Amit Ramani in 2014. Multiple VC funding rounds (Sequoia, Nexus Venture Partners, Peak XV/Sequoia Growth) diluted the promoter progressively. Post-IPO, promoter holding is just 17% — the lowest of any stock in Garvit's portfolio. Compare to Bondada Engineering (45%), Borana Weaves (65%), DEE Development (70%). This low promoter holding means: (a) Management doesn't have "skin in the game" proportional to their responsibility, (b) Institutional investors (VCs) who hold large stakes can exit at any time, creating overhang, (c) Future dilution (ESOPs, potential QIPs) is more likely. The stock's -52% fall from ₹719 to ₹348 partly reflects VC overhang as early investors seek exits at various price points.
1
CFO resigned February 2, 2026 — governance signal to watch.
CFO Ravi Dugar resigned effective February 2, 2026, for "career opportunities." New CFO Sumit Rochlani appointed immediately. CFO departures at recently-listed companies raise questions — was it voluntary departure, board pressure, or disagreement on accounting/strategy? Watch Q4 FY26 results (April/May 2026) closely for any accounting changes, restatements, or unusual items that might explain the CFO departure timing.
2
PAT margins at 4–6% are structurally thin due to Ind AS 116 lease accounting.
Every lease payment to building owners is reclassified under Ind AS 116: the "interest" component reduces PAT, the "depreciation" component reduces PAT. This creates the paradox of 36.5% EBITDA but only 5.7% PAT. The actual cash generation is better than PAT suggests — but the thin PAT means any surprise (higher interest rates, lease renegotiation) can swing the company from profitable to loss-making in one quarter. This accounting structure creates permanent uncertainty around "real" profitability.
3
Seat addition guidance cut from 40K to 32K — growth is decelerating.
FY25 added 40,000 seats. FY26 is adding only 32,000 (20% fewer). The reason given (discipline at peak real estate prices) is intellectually sound — but it also means revenue growth will slow from 30%+ to potentially 20–25% in FY27. For a company trading at 37x EBITDA, decelerating growth is the key valuation risk. If growth slows to 15% in FY28 as the market matures, the multiple should compress significantly.
4
India commercial real estate is at a cyclical peak — rents rising.
India's office market (Mumbai, Bengaluru, NCR) has seen significant rent increases in FY24–FY26 driven by IT sector demand, MNC India expansion, and limited Grade A supply. This is good for Awfis's revenue (higher realisation per seat) but bad for new center economics (signing leases at peak prices = lower ROI on new centers). This is why management reduced seat additions — they see risk in signing expensive new leases at current prices. If rates correct, it opens expansion opportunities.
+
