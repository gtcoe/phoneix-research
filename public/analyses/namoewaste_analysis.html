Understanding the EPR credit market — Namo's hidden revenue engine
What is an EPR credit?
Under India's E-Waste (Management) Rules 2022, every producer/importer of electronics is legally required to collect and recycle a minimum % of the e-waste they generate. If a company cannot do this itself, it purchases EPR certificates from CPCB-authorized recyclers like Namo. EPR credits are priced at ₹8–₹25 per kg depending on the type of material. As of January 2026, MSTC has been selected to launch a formal exchange for EPR certificate trading, creating a regulated market that benefits organized recyclers like Namo. The mandatory 60% recycling target creates a USD 65 million annual EPR certificate market. Rising to 80% by FY28 will further expand this market. This is a regulation-created, recurring revenue stream that doesn't depend on commodity prices.

What Namo eWaste actually does — the full business explained
India generates 3.8 million tonnes of e-waste annually — the world's 3rd largest generator. Less than 20% is recycled formally. Every year, more electronics are produced, more old ones are discarded, and regulation forces producers to fund formal recycling. Namo sits at the centre of this structural shift from informal to formal recycling.
ITAD — highest-margin, stickiest business
+
What it is: When Google or Samsung replaces 10,000 laptops, they can't just trash them. Data needs destruction certification. Namo collects, destroys data (DoD/NIST standards), and either refurbishes for resale or recycles.
+
Why it's sticky: Corporate clients need auditable chain-of-custody for data security compliance. Once Namo is their certified partner, switching is painful — new vendor needs all the same certifications and integrations.
+
Revenue model: Fee per asset + refurbishment revenue from resale of functional hardware + scrap metal value. Triple revenue per pickup vs simple recycling.
EPR compliance — the regulation-powered stream
→
E-Waste Rules 2022: All EEE producers must meet 60% collection targets. Rising to 80% by FY28. Penalties for non-compliance: ₹5,000–₹20,000 per tonne. Companies buy EPR credits from Namo instead of building own infrastructure.
+
MSTC exchange (Jan 2026): India's first EPR certificate trading exchange went live. This creates price discovery, liquidity, and a formal market for credits — Namo is a direct beneficiary as an authorized recycler.
+
Battery EPR: 70% Li-ion battery recovery mandated by FY28. Namo's Nashik plant is now one of the few authorized battery recyclers. Battery EPR credit prices are higher than e-waste credits.
The 90%+ women-led workforce — a genuine competitive advantage, not just optics
This is not a PR stunt. E-waste dismantling is detailed, precision work. Women workers in India's industrial areas accept lower wages for the same quality output. Namo's 90%+ women workforce creates a structural cost advantage of 15–25% vs competitors using mixed or predominantly male workforces. This explains why EBITDA margins are improving even as revenue scales — the operating cost base is structurally lean.

The 5 structural moats
1
CPCB authorization as e-waste and battery recycler: This is a government-granted licence to operate. Getting authorization requires facility inspections, equipment certification, and process audits. A new competitor can't start overnight — minimum 12–18 months of regulatory process before first tonne processed.
2
Data destruction certifications (DoD/NIST): Corporate ITAD clients require certified data destruction to protect against data breach liability. These certifications require facility-level audits, specific equipment, and documented chain-of-custody. Namo's certifications are a verifiable compliance moat for its ITAD business.
3
First-mover in Li-ion battery recycling (organized sector): Nashik plant positions Namo as one of very few authorized battery recyclers in India as EV penetration begins. The regulatory EPR framework for batteries is being set up precisely when Namo has installed capacity — a timing advantage.
4
ICP-MS laboratory for high-purity metal recovery: Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry ensures Namo can achieve and verify high-purity copper/aluminium recovery. This enables selling directly to industrial buyers at a premium vs mixed scrap.
5
Network effect of 26+ collection centres: Each collection centre makes Namo more convenient for more geographies, enabling more client acquisitions, enabling more centres — a genuine network moat that grows stronger as the company scales.
Risk analysis — what can go wrong
1
Working capital days exploded from 83.5 → 125 days — this is the most critical red flag.
This means Namo is getting paid much more slowly by clients. At ₹134 Cr revenue, each 10-day increase in debtor days = ~₹4 Cr more working capital needed. Going from 83.5 to 125 days = ₹17 Cr more tied up in receivables. For a company with ₹10 Cr PAT, this is significant financial stress. If working capital days continue to rise as revenue scales, cash generation deteriorates even as profits look healthy on paper. Watch this every quarter. If it crosses 140 days without explanation — reduce position.
2
SME board illiquidity — same structural constraint as Grand Continent.
NAMOEWASTE trades on NSE SME. Average daily traded value is thin — perhaps ₹1–3 Cr on good days. If you need to exit a ₹30–50K position quickly, you may face 5–8% bid-ask spread and partial fills. Treat this as a locked-in position for 3+ years. Not a tradeable position. The stock fell from ATH ₹284 to ₹127 (-55%) in 8 months — if you panic at the lows, illiquidity means you sell at the worst price.
3
CPCB certification renewal dependency.
Namo's entire business requires valid "Authorised E-Waste Recycler" certifications from CPCB (Central Pollution Control Board). These need periodic renewal. If certification lapses or is revoked (for any regulatory violation), operations halt. A company with 60 employees and rapid expansion has elevated compliance execution risk. Any safety incident or environmental violation at a new plant could trigger scrutiny.
4
Commodity metal price sensitivity.
Approximately 35% of revenue comes from selling recovered metals (copper, aluminium, iron). These prices fluctuate with global commodity cycles. A 20% drop in copper prices cuts this revenue stream by 7% of total revenue — a material impact on an 8% PAT margin business. Hydrometallurgy products (Li, Ni, Co, Mn) have even higher price volatility — battery metal prices crashed 40–60% in 2023 and could do so again.
5
Hyderabad plant execution risk — the next capacity catalyst is unproven.
The 25,000 MTPA Hyderabad plant is the next revenue growth driver. If it opens 6–9 months late (which is common for Indian industrial projects), FY26 revenue growth slows materially. Additionally, a new city means new logistics, new supplier relationships, and new local regulatory approvals — all of which can delay and inflate costs.
6
Small team for ambitious plans (60 employees).
A 60-person company is running 4 plants across 3 states, serving global MNCs, managing CPCB compliance, and planning hydrometallurgy. Management bandwidth is a genuine constraint. The March 2026 resignation of an independent director and board restructuring (new Chairman appointed) adds governance uncertainty during a growth phase.
+
Major mitigants:
Promoter holding 69% (zero pledge). IPO proceeds fully funding expansion without debt risk. OCF turned positive in H1 FY26 — self-funding business model emerging. Regulatory tailwind from E-Waste Rules 2022 creates demand that compounds every year. Government cleared USD 180 million incentive for critical minerals recycling (Jun 2025) — Namo's hydrometallurgy plan directly benefits.
