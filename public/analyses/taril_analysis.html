Revenue & profit — breakout performance FY25, consolidation FY26, acceleration ahead
⚡ HVDC milestone — first Indian private company to win HVDC order from PGCIL
In December 2025, TARIL received a ₹53.33 Cr order from PGCIL for repair, erection, testing and commissioning of a 397 MVA HVDC Converter Transformer — making it the first Indian private sector company to win such a contract. This is not just a ₹53 Cr order — it is a qualification milestone. Once TARIL successfully completes this HVDC repair work, PGCIL can formally approve TARIL as a supplier for new HVDC transformers — the highest-value, highest-barrier segment of India's power equipment market. HVDC corridors (for renewable energy transmission) require transformers worth ₹5,000–50,000 Cr over the next decade. TARIL aims to qualify for indigenous HVDC technology by 2027. This one order opens the door to a market that previously belonged exclusively to ABB/Siemens/Hitachi Energy and Quality Power.

What TARIL makes — the product stack from ₹2 Cr to ₹50 Cr per unit
TARIL is India's largest private sector facility for the widest range of transformers. Unlike most competitors who specialise in 1–2 types, TARIL makes power, furnace, rectifier, traction, solar, and now HVDC transformers. This breadth gives TARIL access to multiple demand cycles simultaneously — when one segment slows, others compensate.
Product portfolio — six transformer categories
1
Power transformers (up to 500 MVA, 1200 kV): Core product. Stepup/stepdown transformers for power generation and transmission. PGCIL, NTPC, State Discoms, IPPs are primary customers. Revenue contribution: ~50–55% of total. Highest volume product.
2
Furnace transformers (EAF/LF): Electric arc furnace transformers for steel (Tata Steel, JSW, SAIL) and aluminium smelters. TARIL exported one of the largest-ever EAF transformers globally (220/253 MVA). Higher ASP, fewer units — premium segment. Revenue: ~20–25%.
3
Rectifier transformers: DC power supply for electrochemical processes — chlorine, aluminium, copper refining. Industrial niche with limited competition. Revenue: ~10%.
+
HVDC transformers (NEW — FY26 breakthrough): ₹53.33 Cr PGCIL HVDC repair order — first Indian private company. HVDC transformers for long-distance DC transmission links. If qualification secured by 2027, opens access to India's entire HVDC renewable corridor buildout. ASP: ₹15–50 Cr per unit vs ₹1–5 Cr for standard power transformers.
+
Specialty: Traction, Solar, Green Hydrogen, Mobile substations: Railway traction transformers for Vande Bharat + electrification. Solar substation transformers. Green hydrogen electrolysers. Mobile/containerised substations for rapid deployment. Together: ~10–15% of revenue, growing fastest.
Backward integration — the margin unlock strategy
→
Currently importing: CRGO steel, CTC conductors (transposed copper wire), transformer bushings, pressboard. These 4 components account for ~30% of production cost. India has very few domestic suppliers, making TARIL dependent on imports (exchange rate risk, lead time risk).
+
Backward integration target: By Q1 FY28, TARIL aims to manufacture CTC, bushings, pressboard, CRGO processing and tank fabrication in-house. This eliminates the import margin (supplier markup) and foreign exchange volatility. Estimated margin improvement: 200–300 basis points = EBITDA margin moving from 15% to 17–18%.
!
Timeline has slipped: Originally targeted Q1 FY26. Then Q1 FY27. Now Q1 FY28. Each delay pushes the margin expansion thesis by one year. The Moraiya capacity expansion also slipped 4–5 months to Q2 FY27. Execution delays are a pattern — not a one-time event.

Guidance analysis — the most important concern: management has missed revenue guidance significantly
🚨 Major guidance miss: ₹3,500 Cr guided for FY26 → ₹2,395 Cr actual (-31%)
At Q4 FY25 results (exit of FY25), management projected FY26 revenue of ~₹3,500 Cr. At Q1 FY26, this was reaffirmed. At Q2 FY26, it was suddenly revised down to ₹2,600 Cr. The actual FY26 was ₹2,395 Cr — missing even the revised guidance by 8%. This is a material credibility issue. The primary reasons given: (1) Selective order booking policy — TARIL deliberately chose not to take lower-margin orders, (2) Supply chain delays — copper custom clearance delays impacted production in Q1–Q2, (3) Site readiness — customer sites not ready for transformer delivery in Q2. Management argues these were conscious choices, not demand weakness. Critically: the ₹23,000 Cr inquiry pipeline confirms demand is not the problem. But investors must discount ALL future TARIL revenue guidance by 20–30% and build scenarios accordingly.
✓ FY26 production milestone — 33,000 MVA highest ever
Despite the revenue miss, TARIL achieved its highest-ever annual transformer production at 33,000 MVA (vs 29,118 MVA in FY25 — the previous record). This is important: the company CAN manufacture at increasing scale. The revenue miss was a revenue recognition/delivery timing issue (some finished goods were pending delivery due to heavy rains and site issues) — not a production capability failure. Production capacity is growing as planned.
Production credibility: 8/10. Manufacturing capability is genuine and improving.
⚠ FY27 guidance: Management "optimistic about ₹5,000 Cr with 18–19% OPM"
Management's FY27 target is ~₹5,000 Cr revenue at 18–19% EBITDA. Given the pattern of FY26 miss, apply a 20–30% discount to this guidance. Base case FY27: ₹3,500–4,000 Cr revenue at 16–17% EBITDA. This still represents 46–67% revenue growth — substantial even at the discounted level. The Moraiya capacity (22,000 MVA) coming online in Q2 FY27 is critical — without it, ₹4,000+ Cr revenue is very difficult.
Revenue credibility (discounted): 6/10. Apply 20–30% haircut to management guidance on revenue. Margin guidance (15–17%) is more credible — FY26 delivered 15.1%, on track for 16–17%.
$1 Billion (~₹8,300 Cr) revenue target — 3 years from FY26
Management has consistently outlined the path to $1 Billion revenue (≈₹8,300 Cr) over 3 years from FY26 = by FY29. With 75,000 MVA capacity (when Moraiya completes), full backward integration, HVDC capability, and sustained 15% India transformer market CAGR, ₹8,000+ Cr by FY29 is directionally credible — but requires zero further execution delays and sustained order inflows. The ₹23,000 Cr inquiry pipeline provides demand confidence; execution consistency is the risk. Apply 25–30% probability to the ₹8,300 Cr FY29 target being hit exactly as stated. A more realistic scenario: ₹6,000–7,000 Cr by FY29.
Credibility: 5/10 on the specific $1B FY29 target. Directionally sound — actual achievement likely ₹5,500–7,000 Cr by FY29 at current execution pace.

Risks — the full, honest picture
1
Management guidance credibility — 31% revenue miss FY26 is serious.
This is the most important risk and it's not about the business — it's about trusting the numbers management provides. When a company guides ₹3,500 Cr and delivers ₹2,395 Cr, every future guidance must be treated with significant skepticism. This doesn't mean the business is bad — it means investors need to build more conservative scenarios and not pay a high multiple on management projections. The selective order booking explanation is partially valid but partially convenient — a 31% miss is very large regardless of justification.
2
CEO resigned January 2026 — MD Mamtora takes over directly.
CEO Mukul Srivastava resigned effective January 7, 2026. MD Satyen Mamtora (second-generation promoter, 30+ years experience) became CEO from January 8. This concentration of power in the promoter-CEO role is both a strength (alignment) and a risk (no external professional management layer). Srivastava's reason for departure was not publicly disclosed — investors should monitor whether this is a one-time event or signals broader management friction.
3
Capacity expansion delays are becoming a pattern.
Original timeline: 15,000 MVA Changodar → Q4 FY24 → Actual: Jun 2025 (5+ quarters delay). 22,000 MVA Moraiya → Q4 FY26 → Now: Q2 FY27 (4–5 month delay). Backward integration → Q1 FY26 → Q1 FY27 → Q1 FY28 (2-year delay). Each delay pushes revenue recognition and the margin expansion thesis. The delays are genuine (plant & machinery delivery issues) but the recurrence is concerning. Every delay means revenue guidance must be further discounted.
4
World Bank debarment — legacy issue requiring resolution.
TARIL was debarred from World Bank-funded projects (from a 2020 contract dispute). World Bank gave time till January 2026 for explanation. Management responded, calling it a governance issue on the World Bank's side. Resolution status: unclear. If resolved: removes overhang. If escalated: prevents bidding on World Bank-funded projects (significant for export markets). Need to track the outcome in FY27 disclosures.
5
Input cost volatility — CRGO steel and copper are commodity prices.
Transformers are ~60–70% copper and CRGO electrical steel by value. Both are traded global commodities with significant price volatility. In H1 FY26, copper custom delays impacted production. Global copper supply/demand (EV adoption, AI data centres) is creating structural price pressure. While TARIL passes through commodity costs in contracts, fixed-price older contracts and timing mismatches create quarterly margin volatility. The backward integration plan (CRGO processing in-house) addresses this but is 2 years away.
+
Genuine strengths:
India's largest private transformer manufacturer by range and capacity. 30+ years heritage — trusted by PGCIL, NTPC, major industrials. HVDC breakthrough — opens the highest-margin transformer segment. ₹23,000 Cr inquiry pipeline = demand is not the problem. 75,000 MVA capacity when complete. QIP ₹500 Cr raised — institutional validation. 7–10 year sustainable demand confirmed by management (15% India transformer CAGR). Export growing toward 10%+ of revenue (USD revenue hedges INR weakness). PE at 15–16x is cheap relative to sector peers.

India power transformer sector — the competitive landscape
India's transformer market is a well-structured oligopoly with 5–7 dominant players across different size classes. The market is growing at 15% CAGR with structural demand from T&D capex, Green Energy Corridor, and Railways. TARIL occupies the premium private-sector tier — competing directly with Voltamp, CG Power, and ABB India for high-voltage orders, while competing with BHEL (PSU) for government utilities.

Quality comparison
