🖥️ Why Anant Raj is more than just a real estate company
Anant Raj was a simple NCR real estate developer for 50 years. But since 2022, it has been quietly building a data centre business using its unique asset — 320 acres of debt-free land inside Delhi-NCR, the most sought-after geography for Indian data centres. Land is the primary bottleneck for data centre development in NCR. Anant Raj already owns it. This land moat, combined with the AI/cloud infrastructure boom, is the reason institutional investors have re-rated this stock from a sleepy real estate company to a technology infrastructure play. The 9M FY26 data centre EBITDA contribution of 75% of absolute EBITDA from just ₹58 Cr of revenue shows how high-margin this segment is — and how transformative it becomes at 117 MW (FY28) or 307 MW (FY32).

Why NCR data centre land is a genuine moat
+
Delhi-NCR is India's #1 data centre market for latency-sensitive workloads: Proximity to the country's largest enterprise cluster, government cloud, and India's highest concentration of hyperscaler clients (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud, Oracle) makes NCR land unique. Any new entrant must either buy expensive land (₹10–50 Cr/acre in Manesar/Gurugram) or go to less preferred locations. Anant Raj already has this land at zero marginal cost — it's been on the balance sheet for decades.
+
Data centres command premium pricing over real estate: An acre of Manesar land used for residential development generates ~₹20–40 Cr/year of revenue (once monetised). The same acre used for a data centre generates ₹50–150 Cr/year in recurring revenue at full utilisation. Every MW converted from "residential land" to "data centre" is a 3–5x revenue multiplier on the same asset — permanently.
!
The ROCE trap: ROCE is low (6.7%) because most of the 320 acres is still sitting idle on the balance sheet at historical cost. As data centres go live and the land is monetised, ROCE improves dramatically. The current low ROCE reflects the "before" state — it is not a reflection of the business quality but of the timing of the land-to-DC conversion.

How the two businesses fund each other
The genius of Anant Raj's model: Residential real estate generates consistent cash flows (₹400–500 Cr annual PAT). This cash is being channelled into data centre capex — building MW capacity without needing external debt. When the QIP (₹1,100 Cr) was raised in October 2025, it was specifically to accelerate the data centre buildout from 63 MW to 117 MW. The residential business is effectively the "venture funding engine" for the data centre business — an internal capital recycling loop that external-only DC companies cannot replicate.

Risks — the honest picture for a transitioning company
⚠ ROCE of 6.7% is a structural concern that must be addressed. ROCE = Return on Capital Employed. At 6.7%, Anant Raj is generating just ₹6.70 for every ₹100 of capital deployed — well below its cost of capital (~10–12%). This happens because 320 acres of expensive NCR land sits "idle" on the balance sheet while generating minimal income. The data centre pivot aims to fix this — converting idle land to high-return DC assets. But until DC is at 100+ MW, ROCE stays below 10%. Investors buying on data centre hopes must accept this fundamental inefficiency during the transition. A PE-financed pure DC company would not carry this drag.
1
Capital intensity of 307 MW plan is the biggest risk — ₹15,000 Cr needed.
The full $2.1 billion (₹18,000 Cr) plan requires financing well beyond current cash generation. ₹700 Cr deployed so far. ₹1,100 Cr QIP raised. Residential generates ₹400–500 Cr/year. DC at 63 MW generates ₹300–400 Cr/year. Total FY27 generation: ~₹700–900 Cr. Need: ₹2,500 Cr/year for 6 years to fund 307 MW. Gap: ~₹1,600–1,800 Cr/year requires debt (leverage risk) or additional QIPs (dilution risk). Management has not detailed the full funding plan. This is the elephant in the room for the long-term thesis.
2
Competition from Adani, Hiranandani, NTT, and established DC operators.
Anant Raj is a late entrant to data centres versus Hiranandani (NXTRA Data), CtrlS, STT GDC (Singapore, India-focused), NTT, and others. These players have 10–20 years of DC operational experience vs Anant Raj's 2 years. More critically, hyperscalers (AWS, Google, Azure) are building their own data centres in India — potentially reducing colocation demand over time. Anant Raj's "50% lower cost" moat may compress as competition intensifies and economies of scale are achieved by larger operators.
3
Real estate cycle risk — NCR luxury could overheat.
NCR residential (especially Gurugram luxury) has been on fire in FY24–FY26. Anant Raj's luxury projects (The Estate One, Project Navya with Birla) are priced ₹2–5 Cr per unit. If interest rates rise or luxury supply exceeds demand, pre-sales slow and residential cashflows (which fund DC capex) compress. This is the "kill switch" on the data centre funding engine.
4
QIP dilution already happened — ₹1,100 Cr at ₹695 floor (Oct 2025).
The QIP was raised when the stock was near its 52-week high (₹744). Current price ~₹498 means QIP investors are sitting on -28% paper loss. This creates overhang — institutional investors who bought at ₹695 may look to exit at any meaningful recovery, creating supply pressure every time the stock approaches ₹650–700. This is a technical headwind for 12–18 months.
5
Utilisation risk — DC revenue depends on lease-up pace.
Data centres generate revenue only when capacity is leased. Building 35 MW of new DC infrastructure (28 → 63 MW) is the easy part — finding enterprise and cloud clients to fill it at target rates within 12–18 months of commissioning is harder. Rent-free periods (typically 3–6 months post-commissioning) mean each new facility takes time to generate full revenue. If utilisation stays below 60%, the revenue per MW is lower than management's targets.
