What Borana makes — B2B upstream fabric supplier
Borana sits at the upstream end of India's textile value chain. It makes unbleached greige (grey) fabric — the raw, unprocessed material that goes to dyehouses, printers, and finishers before becoming the colourful apparel or home textile you buy. This is B2B industrial manufacturing, not consumer branding. Borana's customers are processors and traders, not end consumers. This explains its high margins (no marketing spend, no inventory at retail) and high ROE (asset-light receivables from B2B).
The product: synthetic greige fabric
1
Polyester Oriented Yarn (POY) → PTY Yarn → Greige Fabric: Borana buys POY yarn (petroleum derivative), texturises it in-house to make PTY yarn, then weaves PTY yarn into greige (unbleached) fabric using water jet looms. Greige is then sold to processors who dye/print/finish it for end use.
2
End applications: Apparel (fashion, activewear, sarees, dress material), home textiles (bed sheets, curtains), technical textiles (tent fabrics, industrial uses), traditional textiles. The fabric is versatile — the same material serves multiple end markets depending on the processing.
3
Why water jet looms: Water jet looms produce polyester/synthetic fabric more efficiently than shuttle or rapier looms. Higher speed (800–1,200 picks/minute), lower energy per meter, minimal waste, consistent quality. Borana has 700+ high-speed water jet looms — purpose-built for synthetic fabrics. This technology choice is the core competitive advantage.
4
98% repeat orders — why customers stick: Greige fabric quality consistency is critical for downstream processors. If quality varies, their dyeing/printing runs produce inconsistent output. Borana's high repeat rate reflects consistent quality — a critical moat in this segment.
Why the margins are high and sustainable
+
Vertical backward integration (PTY manufacturing): Most Surat greige manufacturers buy PTY yarn from traders. Borana makes its own — capturing the yarn trader's 3–5% margin. This one factor alone is responsible for ~3–5% of the margin differential vs peers.
+
No marketing, no branding, no retail inventory risk: Borana sells to B2B customers who come back for the same quality. No advertising spend. No celebrity endorsements (unlike Cellecor). No retail shelf competition. Opex is purely manufacturing — fabric in, fabric out.
+
Scale economies at Sachin cluster: Three/four units in the same industrial estate. Shared infrastructure. Management team physically on-site. Labour pool available. Minimises diseconomies of scale as it grows.
!
The commodity risk: POY yarn is a petroleum-derived product. Crude oil price → polyester price → POY yarn cost → Borana's input cost. In FY24, POY prices actually fell (helping margins). But a sustained crude oil rally compresses margins. Borana has no long-term supply agreements — spot market purchases only.
India's man-made fabric (MMF) opportunity — why the sector tailwind is real
+
India's MMF penetration is rising rapidly: India has historically favoured cotton. But polyester/synthetic fabrics are growing at 12–15% CAGR vs 4–6% for cotton. Reasons: price advantage (polyester 30–40% cheaper), performance (moisture-wicking, quick-dry, durability), and fashion preference shift. MMF now ~35% of India's fibre consumption, rising to ~50% by 2030.
+
China+1 sourcing shift: Global apparel brands shifting fabric sourcing away from China. India, Vietnam, Bangladesh are beneficiaries. Surat's greige fabric cluster — India's largest — is positioned to capture this export demand. Borana's current production is primarily domestic but export diversification is the next growth lever.
+
India textile & apparel market: $190 Bn by FY26 at 10% CAGR. Domestic demand from rising middle class + urbanisation is the primary driver. Government's Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme for textiles provides additional policy tailwind for manufacturers who invest in capacity.

Risks — the full, honest picture for an upstream B2B manufacturer
This is one of the cleanest risk profiles of all stocks analysed this session. There are no promoter pledge issues, no negative FCF, no governance red flags, no CFO change, no overseas concentration. The risks are primarily business risks — commodity exposure, sector competitiveness, and scale concentration.
1
POY yarn = crude oil derivative — primary input cost risk.
Borana's primary raw material is POY (Polyester Oriented Yarn), made from polyester chips, which come from PTA and MEG — both petroleum derivatives. When crude oil prices rise, POY costs rise, and Borana's 20% EBITDA margins can compress to 14–16%. The company has no long-term supply agreements (all spot purchases). Top 10 suppliers account for 90%+ of raw material purchases — some concentration. Mitigation: Borana's PTY in-house manufacture provides partial buffer (captures yarn trader margin even when input cost rises).
2
Greige fabric is a commodity — limited pricing power.
Borana sells grey, unbranded, undifferentiated fabric. While its quality consistency (98% repeat orders) provides some stickiness, in a glut scenario (too many Surat weavers adding capacity), greige fabric realisations could fall from ₹1.05/m to ₹0.85–0.90/m — compressing margins significantly. The India MMF sector is growing, but so is installed capacity. Monitor realisation per meter quarterly.
3
Working capital days jumped 33 → 61 — must be tracked.
This is the most recent material concern. As Unit 4B ramped up, Borana needed more inventory (yarn buffer) and extended receivables (customers taking time to absorb higher volumes). If this normalises to 35–45 days post-ramp-up, the ROE stays near 45%. If it persists at 60+ days, effective ROE falls to ~30%, and the high-ROE investment thesis partially breaks. Watch this every quarter.
4
Young company — 4 years old as a formal entity (incorporated 2020).
Borana Weaves Pvt Ltd was incorporated in October 2020. Listed in May 2025. The company has only 4 years of operating history as a formal corporate entity, and just 1 year as a listed company. While the promoter family has 25+ years of textile experience, the formal governance structures (audit committee, board independence, institutional oversight) are new and largely untested. This is inherently higher risk than a 20-year-old listed company.
5
Geographic concentration — all plants in Surat, Gujarat.
Every manufacturing unit is in Sachin industrial estate, Surat. A natural disaster, extreme weather event, power disruption, or regulatory issue affecting Surat's industrial zone affects 100% of Borana's output. This is typical for Indian small-cap manufacturers but worth acknowledging.
+
Genuine mitigants — why risks are manageable:
No promoter pledge. Zero OFS in IPO — all proceeds for business. IPO monitoring: zero deviations. Earnings calls held regularly (unlike Indo Tech). D/E declining (0.43x and reducing). Promoter 65.2% — fully aligned. 98% repeat orders from stable B2B customer base. Energy cost reduction via solar (reducing oil price sensitivity). Unit 4B fully commissioned. Revenue growth is mechanically tied to loom count (predictable). New unit commissioning pace (one per year) gives visibility on growth trajectory.
