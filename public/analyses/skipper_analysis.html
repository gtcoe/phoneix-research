Three business segments — understanding Skipper's full picture
Skipper has three segments that serve different markets but share manufacturing infrastructure. The synergy: the same steel fabrication plants that make transmission towers also make telecom towers, railway structures, and polymer pipe extrusion lines. This operational flexibility across segments is a genuine competitive advantage — capacity can be reallocated to whichever segment has the highest demand.
⚡ Engineering — T&D Structures (core, ~70% of revenue)
Transmission towers · Monopoles · EPC · Export supply · Railway structures
Skipper manufactures power transmission and distribution structures — the steel lattice towers and monopoles that carry electricity across India's grid. Three sub-segments: (1) Supply: manufacturing and supplying towers to utilities (PGCIL, SEBs). Lower revenue per tonne but predictable. (2) EPC: engineering, procurement, AND construction — Skipper does the civil work too. Higher revenue, similar or lower EBITDA per rupee. Currently executing ~5,000 circuit km. (3) Export: supplies to 65+ countries (Australia, Middle East, North America, Africa). USD-denominated, premium pricing. H1 FY26 export +27% YoY. New subsidiaries: UAE (Abu Dhabi), USA (Texas), Brazil (São Paulo, LATAM LTDA).
H1 FY26: Highest-ever revenue; export +27% YoY to ₹523 Cr
🌊 Polymer — Pipes & Fittings (growing, ~25% of revenue)
CPVC · UPVC · Agriculture pipes · Plumbing · Marina brand (bath fittings)
Skipper is a significant polymer pipe manufacturer — CPVC, UPVC, HDPE pipes for agriculture irrigation and building plumbing. Brand name: 'Skipper' (pipes) + 'Marina' (bath fittings, water storage tanks). Distribution: 70,000+ retail touchpoints across India. This segment competes directly with Astral, Supreme Industries, Prince Pipes — established premium players. Skipper is positioned as a value player with competitive pricing. The polymer segment provides geographic diversification (sells across India via retail) vs Engineering (project-based, lumpy). Margin profile: ~12–15% EBITDA — slightly better than Engineering.

The EPC pivot — highest revenue but execution-intensive
EPC (Engineering + Procurement + Construction)
+
Why EPC is strategically critical: EPC converts Skipper from a "tower supplier" to a "project executor" — the distinction that separates commodity manufacturers from premium contractors. PGCIL prefers EPC vendors for large 765 kV projects (fewer contractors to manage, single-point accountability). EPC orders are typically 30–50% larger than pure supply orders. Market perception: EPC = higher quality, higher trust.
!
EPC challenges: Higher working capital intensity (must manage civil contractors, land approvals, right-of-way). Execution risk — project delays can compress margin. Skipper currently executing 5,000+ circuit km — at this scale, even 1% project slippage is meaningful. Management's Q1 FY26 comment: "exceptional operational execution" suggests it's going well.
Substation EPC — new high-value entry
+
Skipper diversifying into civil and electrical components within T&D: FY26 saw diversification into civil (foundations, civil structures) and electrical components (switchgear, control panels) within T&D projects. This further captures the project value chain — from towers (structural) to full substation (electrical + civil + structural). As a complete EPC contractor, Skipper commands higher ASP and better client stickiness.
+
Second Tower Testing Station commissioned: India's largest Tower Monopole Load Testing Station was India's first (2020). The second test bed facility further differentiates Skipper — utilities (PGCIL, Middle East, Australia) require tower certification before procurement. Having in-house testing accelerates approval cycles and is a genuine competitive moat.

PAT margin at 3.1–3.6% is thin — interest coverage may be a concern.
Skipper's PAT margin of 3.1% (FY26: ₹145 Cr on ₹4,644 Cr revenue) is thin for a ₹5,129 Cr market cap company. This is structural for capital goods manufacturers with significant steel pass-through in EPC. Screener flags "company's cost of borrowing seems high." The Rights Issue raised ₹199 Cr — suggesting the company needed external equity. Watch the debt-to-equity ratio carefully. If EPC ramp-up requires more working capital debt without proportional EBITDA growth, interest coverage could fall further.
2
Steel price volatility directly impacts margins.
Transmission towers are predominantly steel. Steel prices fluctuate 20–30% annually. Most supply contracts have price-escalation clauses (protecting Skipper if steel rises), but the timing of cost recovery vs cost incurrence creates quarterly margin volatility. EPC contracts are more complex — partial escalation clauses mean Skipper bears some steel price risk. In FY22 (high steel prices), PAT margin was ~2%; in FY24 (lower steel), it recovered. This cyclicality is structural and unavoidable.
3
Market cap ₹5,129 Cr — 3x needs ₹15,387 Cr. Requires PAT of ₹700+ Cr.
For Garvit's 3x-in-3-years goal, Skipper at ₹5,129 Cr needs to reach ₹15,387 Cr. At 20x PE, this requires PAT of ₹769 Cr. Skipper's FY26 PAT is ₹145.8 Cr. Getting to ₹769 Cr requires 5.3x PAT growth in 3 years — which implies a 74% annual PAT growth rate from FY26. Even in the bull case (FY28 PAT ₹520 Cr at 22x PE = ₹11,440 Cr), 3x is not achievable in 3 years. 2x is more realistic by FY27–FY28. This is the fundamental math problem at the current market cap.
4
ROE at 9.72% (3-year avg) is low relative to quality expectations.
Low ROE reflects: (1) Large capex cycles (building tower manufacturing plants requires significant capital), (2) Working capital locked in EPC projects (6–18 month billing cycles), (3) Polymer segment's distribution working capital. As EPC projects complete and receivables are collected, ROE should improve toward 15%+ by FY28. But until then, the stock trades on growth expectations rather than current returns — making it valuation-sensitive to any guidance miss.

T&D structure + EPC sector landscape — where Skipper sits
India's power transmission sector is a concentrated quality oligopoly. Four Indian companies dominate: KEC International (₹25,000+ Cr mcap), Kalpataru Projects (₹20,000+ Cr), Sterlite Power (unlisted), and Skipper (₹5,129 Cr). All four are beneficiaries of India's ₹3.5 lakh Cr T&D capex plan by 2032. The difference: KEC and Kalpataru are primarily EPC contractors; Skipper is unique as a manufacturer-cum-EPC player. This manufacturing integration creates margin differentiation.
