♻️ India's #1 & ONLY BSE-listed e-waste recycler — an irreplaceable regulatory moat
Eco Recycling (Ecoreco) has been India's pioneer in formal e-waste management since 2005 — 20 years before most competitors recognised the opportunity. It is the only BSE-listed pure-play e-waste management company in India. The regulatory moat is extraordinary: to legally recycle e-waste in India, companies must obtain CPCB (Central Pollution Control Board) authorization — a multi-year process. Ecoreco has had this for 20 years and is the go-to name for EPR (Extended Producer Responsibility) compliance across 120+ countries. As India's EPR enforcement tightens (mandatory since 2022, centralised portal 2024, Environmental Compensation provisions for non-compliance), demand for authorised recyclers like Ecoreco grows structurally. What Ecoreco does — six business lines, each with distinct economics
Ecoreco's revenue comes from two fundamentally different sources: (1) Commodity recovery — selling recycled metals, plastics, glass extracted from e-waste (revenue = scrap sales, margin depends on commodity prices), and (2) Services — EPR compliance, data destruction, ITAD, lamp recycling (revenue = service fee, higher and more predictable margin). The services/EPR portion is the high-margin engine; commodity recovery is the bulk volume driver.
High-margin services (the moat)
1
EPR compliance services: Extended Producer Responsibility requires electronics manufacturers (Apple, Samsung, Sony, LG, Tata) to ensure their products are recycled at end-of-life. Ecoreco collects, processes, and issues EPR certificates to producers. Revenue: service fee + EPR credit price. As enforcement tightens (Environmental Compensation for non-compliance), every producer must buy EPR credits from authorized recyclers like Ecoreco. This is a regulatory demand — not elastic. Margin: 60–70%.
2
ITAD + Data Destruction: IT Asset Disposition — securely recycling laptops, servers, phones from corporates and data centres. Data destruction certificates required for compliance. Increasingly important as India's data centre boom creates massive end-of-life IT equipment. Very high ASP per unit (₹500–5,000 per device depending on certification level). Margin: 65–75%.
3
Precious Metal Recovery: Circuit boards contain gold (0.03–0.3 gm/kg), silver (3–5 gm/kg), palladium (0.01–0.05 gm/kg). With gold at ₹8,000/gram, this is a high-value revenue stream — especially from smartphones and servers. NITI Aayog estimated India's e-waste contains ₹51,000 Cr of materials. Ecoreco is one of very few companies with the technology to recover these metals at high purity. Margin: very high (near commodity price with low processing cost).
Volume-driven segments (growing)
4
Li-ion Battery Recycling (new, transformative): 6,000 MTPA dedicated facility at Vasai. Recovers lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese from spent EV, laptop, phone batteries. MEITY partnership for technology. India imports 100% of these critical minerals — making domestic recovery strategically national-interest. This segment could become Ecoreco's largest revenue driver by FY28 if EV battery volumes ramp as expected.
5
Lamp Recycling: Mercury-containing lamps (CFL, fluorescent) require specialized recycling under Hazardous Waste Rules. Ecoreco is one of very few authorized lamp recyclers. Small segment but high compliance barrier — essentially no competition.
6
BookMyJunk + Recycling on Wheels (SmartER): Consumer-facing collection app + mobile recycling vans for residential e-waste collection. Mentioned by PM Modi in Mann Ki Baat (national radio). Creates brand awareness and feeds the processing pipeline with consumer e-waste. Scales the informal sector into the formal economy by training and absorbing informal workers.
The EPR framework — the regulatory engine that makes Ecoreco's moat durable
+
EPR is mandatory, non-negotiable, and heavily enforced from 2023: Every company that sells electronics in India must ensure an equivalent weight of e-waste is recycled each year. If they don't, they pay Environmental Compensation (EC) — essentially a fine. This means every major electronics brand (Samsung, Apple, Dell, HP) must buy EPR credits from authorized recyclers like Ecoreco. As more electronics are sold each year, more EPR credits are demanded. Ecoreco's authorized status (20-year CPCB history) is a virtually insurmountable barrier — new entrants take 2–4 years to get authorization and build trust with producers.
+
Centralised EPR portal (2024) = compliance enforcement tightened: The government launched a centralized portal where producers must register their EPR targets and submit evidence of recycling. This removes the ability to get fake/low-quality EPR certificates and drives demand toward reputable, large-scale authorized recyclers like Ecoreco. Each new enforcement measure strengthens Ecoreco's competitive position.
Guidance analysis — management communicates via quarterly earnings releases
Ecoreco management publishes quarterly earnings releases with qualitative commentary. Formal revenue guidance is not provided (typical for small-cap B2G businesses). Analysis relies on capacity expansion announcements, EPR market data, and quarterly trends.
✓ Capacity expansion (FY26): 7,200 → 31,200 MTPA — delivered on internal accruals
Management committed to a 4.3x capacity expansion — from 7,200 MTPA to 31,200 MTPA — all funded through internal accruals (zero debt). This was delivered in FY26. The new 40,000 sq ft Vasai plant includes the 18,000 MTPA e-waste expansion plus the dedicated 6,000 MTPA Li-ion battery facility. Self-funded expansion on a zero-debt balance sheet is a remarkable execution signal. Most infrastructure companies raise debt for expansions; Ecoreco's PAT margins (42–47%) generate enough cash to fund growth organically.
Credibility: 9/10. Delivered the expansion on time and without debt — exactly as committed.
✓ 5-year PAT CAGR of 99% — management has compounded at near-100% annually
This is extraordinary for any company. From FY21 PAT of approximately ₹1–2 Cr to FY26 PAT of ₹22.9 Cr — PAT has grown roughly 10–15x in 5 years. The 99% CAGR is not a one-year spike but a genuine 5-year compounding track record. This reflects the EPR regulation-driven demand growth + pricing power from being the only authorized large-scale recycler + operating leverage as fixed costs spread over growing volumes. The challenge is sustaining this pace as the base grows larger.
Credibility: 8/10. 99% CAGR over 5 years is genuinely exceptional. Maintaining 40–50% growth going forward is more realistic but still very strong.
✗ Q3 FY26 revenue -40% YoY — significant quarterly miss requiring explanation
Q3 FY26 saw a -40% revenue decline vs Q3 FY25. EBITDA fell from ₹6.6 Cr to ₹3.47 Cr YoY. The company attributed this to timing of large contracts — e-waste recycling is inherently lumpy as large producers purchase EPR credits in waves, not uniformly. The Q4 FY26 recovery (+248% PAT YoY) validates the "timing" explanation. However, this lumpiness creates significant risk for investors who assume quarterly linearity. Watch for H1 vs H2 revenue patterns — Ecoreco may be inherently back-weighted (most revenue in Q4 as producers rush to meet year-end EPR compliance targets).
The EPR cycle explanation is plausible: producers comply with annual targets, so Q4 (March year-end) is likely strongest. Verify this pattern in historical data.
⚠ Working capital deterioration: 207 → 321 days — management must explain and resolve
Working capital days have jumped from 207 to 321 in one year — a 55% deterioration. Debtor days rose from 79.9 to 119. For a business where the company collects e-waste (essentially getting paid to take product + material value), this working capital build is unusual. Possible explanation: EPR credit receivables from large producers being paid slowly, or advance purchases of e-waste material that hasn't been sold yet. Management must address this explicitly — if not, it suggests cash flow quality is worse than PAT suggests.
Watch: FY27 Q1 balance sheet. If working capital stays above 300 days, it's structural. If it normalises below 200, Q3 was an anomaly.

Risks — the full, honest picture
Working capital days 207 → 321 (55% deterioration in one year) — the biggest concern. In a capital-light service business, working capital of 321 days means the company has nearly a full year's revenue tied up in receivables and inventory. At ₹48 Cr revenue, 321 days WC = ~₹42 Cr locked up. For a company generating ₹22.9 Cr PAT, this is almost the entire annual profit in working capital. If this worsens, Ecoreco may need to raise equity (dilution risk) despite being profitable. The warrants issue (₹12.2 Cr at ₹408/share) suggests management is already aware of the funding gap. Monitor FY27 Q1 balance sheet very carefully.

While illegal, they are virtually impossible to eliminate quickly. Additionally, as EPR credit demand grows, new authorized recyclers may enter (the 2–4 year authorization process is a barrier but not impenetrable). If EPR credit supply grows faster than demand, prices fall and Ecoreco's margins compress.

E-waste + recycling sector landscape — Ecoreco's competitive position
India's e-waste sector is highly fragmented, dominated by the informal sector (90%+ of volume), with very few formal authorised recyclers. Ecoreco's monopoly as the ONLY BSE-listed company is a genuine advantage — institutional investors, ESG mandates, and large MNC clients (who need publicly auditable recycling partners) naturally gravitate to Ecoreco.
