The RDSS macro tailwind — India's largest infrastructure program, hiding in plain sight
🔌 India RDSS: ₹3.03 lakh Cr to modernise every electricity meter in India by 2032
The Revamped Distribution Sector Scheme (RDSS) is India's ₹3.03 lakh crore programme to transform the electricity distribution network. The core mandate: install 25–31 crore smart meters across India — every household, commercial establishment, and industrial connection. Current progress: ~6.1 crore installed. Target: 25 crore by 2032. Gap = 19 crore more meters = 7 more years of installation at accelerating pace.

Why this is important: India's electricity distribution companies (DISCOMs) were losing ₹90,000 Cr annually to Aggregate Technical & Commercial (AT&C) losses — electricity stolen, billed incorrectly, or lost. Smart meters eliminate this by: (1) Remote reading (no human meter reader = no manipulation), (2) Real-time consumption monitoring, (3) Prepaid billing capability, (4) Remote disconnection/reconnection. The government funds 60% of costs; DISCOMs pay the balance over 10 years. For Genus Power, every RDSS order = 8–10 year revenue visibility.

The expanding opportunity: Post electricity smart meters, India is targeting 12 crore smart GAS meters and a similar scale in WATER meters. Genus is already preparing for both verticals. The total TAM could be 3x the current RDSS programme — a ₹9 lakh Cr infrastructure rollout over 15 years.

The AMISP business model — why it's different from just meter manufacturing
The most important thing to understand about Genus Power: It is NOT a meter manufacturer. It is an AMISP — Advanced Metering Infrastructure Service Provider. This is the difference between selling a product (one-time) and running a long-term concession (8–10 year recurring revenue). The AMISP model is what makes Genus Power structurally different from HPL Electric (pure manufacturer) and why the ₹27,000 Cr order book is an 8–10 year revenue pipeline, not just next year's sales.
1
EPC Phase (Years 1–3, 55–58% of total contract value): Genus finances, procures, installs, and commissions the smart meters across the DISCOM's entire geography. This is the capital-intensive phase — Genus spends money upfront (hence the rising debt) and recognises revenue as milestones are achieved. Operational Go-Live (OGL) is the critical milestone — once achieved, invoicing begins. As of December 2025, 16 AMISP projects covering 2.5 crore smart meters have achieved OGL status. Each OGL project = revenue recognition begins = cash flow starts flowing.
2
O&M Phase (Years 4–10, 42–45% of total contract value): Once installed, Genus operates and maintains the meters for 7–8 more years. This is the annuity phase — recurring monthly payments from the DISCOM regardless of installation activity. As OGL projects scale from 16 to all 30+ AMISP projects, the O&M annuity stream builds to ₹800–1,000 Cr/year by FY28–29. This recurring O&M income is the long-term compounding element that most investors underappreciate. It makes Genus a hybrid manufacturer + infrastructure company.
3
Revenue split — EPC vs O&M today and tomorrow: Currently, 90%+ of revenue is from EPC installation (high-growth phase). As OGL projects scale, O&M becomes a growing share — targeting ₹500+ Cr/year by FY28. O&M revenue is higher-margin (no major additional capex), more predictable, and valued at higher multiples by the market. This shift is the next major re-rating catalyst — markets will value the O&M stream at 15–20x vs capital goods companies at 25–30x, driving a multiple expansion.
+
Why 80% of GIC platform revenue goes to Genus: Genus contributed the AMISP contracts, execution capability, and local market knowledge. GIC contributed the capital. Under the platform structure, Genus retains 80% economic interest — GIC earns a fixed return on its capital. This is highly efficient for Genus: use GIC's $2B balance sheet to fund growth without proportional dilution of Genus's earnings share. As the GIC platform scales, each additional ₹100 of revenue = ₹80 to Genus without Genus bearing the full financing cost.

Risks — the three serious ones that explain the -22% from peak
🚨 RISK 1 — PROMOTER PLEDGE 68.75%: THE MOST SERIOUS CONCERN
Genus Power promoters have pledged 68.75% of their shareholding. This means: if the stock falls below a certain threshold, the pledgee (bank/NBFC) can sell the pledged shares in the open market to recover their loan. This creates a self-fulfilling collapse — stock falls → pledge margin call → shares sold → stock falls more. At 68.75% pledge ratio, Genus Power's stock is particularly vulnerable to any negative trigger (ED investigation, execution miss, debt concern). This is NOT a reason to avoid the stock entirely, but it IS the reason the stock is -22% from peak despite exceptional results. The market applies a "pledge discount." Management has not provided a timeline for reducing pledge. Until pledge falls below 30%, this remains an overhang.
⚠️ RISK 2 — ED INVESTIGATION (December 2024)
The Enforcement Directorate conducted searches at Genus Power in December 2024 relating to corruption charges involving a former Bihar IAS officer (Sanjeev Hans) in connection with a large smart meter contract. Genus cooperated and has not received any formal summons since. However: (1) ED investigations in India can be prolonged — if a formal charge sheet is filed, it could result in contract cancellations, blacklisting, or management distraction. (2) The Goa blacklisting rumor (later quashed by court order) shows how vulnerable the stock is to governance-related news. (3) If the Bihar smart meter contracts are impacted, ₹3,000–5,000 Cr of order book could be at risk. The ED investigation is the single biggest binary risk in the entire investment case.
⚠️ RISK 3 — HIGH DEBT + NEGATIVE CASH FLOW (currently)
Gross debt ₹1,975 Cr as of December 2025. Peak expected: ₹2,100–2,200 Cr in FY27. Cash flow from operations was negative in FY25. Interest costs surged 54% YoY in Q3 FY26. Debt-to-EBITDA ratio of 3.64x (5-year average). While management guides cash flow positive by end-FY27, any execution delay or DISCOM payment slowdown could extend this. The RDSS scheme deadline has already been pushed once (from March 2026 to March 2028) — execution delays are the rule, not the exception in Indian infrastructure.
