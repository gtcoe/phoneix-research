🏥 The PPP model — Asia's largest diagnostics network in government hospitals
Krsnaa operates India's (and one of Asia's) largest government PPP diagnostics network — 190 CT/MRI centers, 140 pathology labs, 1,501 tele-radiology centers, 4,000+ patient collection centers across 18 states. The model: Krsnaa installs equipment and operates diagnostics services inside government hospitals, government pays per-test rates, patients pay subsidised or near-free prices. This is structurally different from private labs — revenue is assured by government contracts (75%+ of revenue) rather than competitive patient acquisition. The PPP model gives Krsnaa access to government hospital patient volumes (millions per year) without marketing costs — but also exposes it to government payment delays (debtor days now 152 days).

The PPP model — how Krsnaa makes money in government hospitals
Krsnaa's PPP model is fundamentally different from all private diagnostics companies. Understanding this is essential — it's why Krsnaa has lower margins than Dr Lal but much more revenue visibility, why it has government receivable problems (but these eventually get paid), and why its growth is driven by contract wins rather than marketing spend.
The PPP contract mechanics
1
Contract award: State government/NHM awards Krsnaa a contract to set up and operate diagnostics services in a government hospital or medical college. Krsnaa installs its equipment (CT scanner, MRI, pathology lab) in the government facility.
2
Revenue model: Government pays Krsnaa a fixed rate per test/scan completed. Rate is pre-determined in the contract (typically discounted vs private market, but high volume compensates). Patients pay ₹0 to a nominal amount — government subsidizes the rest.
3
Volume dynamics: Government hospitals serve millions of patients who previously had no access to diagnostics. Volume ramps as centers mature (12–18 months for new centers to reach mature throughput). At maturity, a single CT center generates ₹5–8 Cr/year revenue at 15–20% EBITDA margin.
4
The receivable challenge: Government pays, but slowly — 90–180 days. This is structural across all government healthcare businesses. Krsnaa's Q3 FY26 strategic pause (recover ₹130 Cr) shows management actively managing this. With ₹430 Cr NCD raised, the company now has capital to bridge government payment gaps without distress.
Business segments — 2026 portfolio
R
Radiology (CT/MRI) — core, ~60% of revenue: 190+ CT/MRI centers. Asia's largest CT/MRI PPP network. Revenue per center: ₹3–8 Cr/year. Q3 FY26 goal: 200+ centers. Maharashtra adding 10 MRI centers in H2 FY26. Operating leverage kicks in at scale — fixed equipment cost spreads over more scans.
P
Pathology labs — growing, ~30% of revenue: 140 labs + 4,000+ collection centers. Rajasthan NHM project: 20 new labs + 300 collection centers (largely operational Q4 FY26). Higher margin than radiology at scale. Ayushman empanelment drives pathology volume.
B
Retail/B2C — small but 8x growth: 3,000+ touch points in Maharashtra, Punjab, Assam, Odisha. Home collection service. Contributing 8% of 9M FY26 revenue. 8x year-on-year growth (very small base). This segment diversifies from government dependency — crucial for long-term margin improvement.
H
Hospital PPP (new) — Apulki Healthcare: 24% stake in Apulki Healthcare. First cancer/cardiac PPP hospital in India (150 beds, Pune, CGHS rates, with Pune Municipal Corporation). Plans: 4 more hospitals, 600 total beds. This segment has the highest long-term potential — hospital PPP is where Krsnaa's model can scale from diagnostics to comprehensive healthcare delivery.
The operating leverage thesis — why FY27 margins should expand materially
+
Fixed cost absorption as new centers mature: Every new CT/MRI center requires ₹3–5 Cr of capital investment and 3–5 people. In Year 1, revenue is low as patient volumes ramp. In Years 2–3, volumes ramp but costs stay fixed → margins expand dramatically. With 190 centers now operational (many commissioned in FY25–26), FY27 is the year the majority enter their mature phase. This is the most powerful near-term catalyst for Krsnaa — and it's not driven by winning new contracts but simply by time.

⚠ NCD ₹430 Cr at 7-year tenure — debt signal requiring careful analysis
In Dec 2025, Krsnaa approved raising ₹430 Cr via unlisted NCDs (semi-annual fixed interest, 7-year tenure). For a company generating ₹80 Cr PAT on ₹717 Cr revenue, raising ₹430 Cr in debt is significant. The company says this is for working capital (bridging government receivables) and project expansion. The positive: it removes the cash flow squeeze from slow government payments. The concern: ₹430 Cr NCD at what interest rate? If 11–13%, annual interest ₹47–56 Cr would consume 50–70% of current PAT — material drag on future earnings.
Watch FY27 Q1 finance costs carefully — NCD interest will be visible in the P&L from Q4 FY26 onwards.

🚨 Major governance red flag: CFO + 3 independent directors resigned in 4 months (Jan–Apr 2026)
CFO Pawan Daga resigned effective January 19, 2026 (cited "personal and professional priorities" after 5 years — unusual timing). Then in April 2026, the board approved three director cessations (multiple independent directors) and appointed an interim CFO from April 17, 2026. This is an extraordinary level of leadership churn in a short period. While each individual departure may have innocuous explanations, the cumulative pattern (CFO + independent directors leaving within months of each other) is a serious governance red flag. It demands scrutiny of: (a) accounting practices, (b) NCD terms and purpose, (c) related party transactions. This is the single most important risk to investigate before any investment.
1
Promoter holding at 27.1% — one of the lowest in the analyzed portfolio.
Promoter holding at 27.1% is dangerously low for a mid-cap healthcare company. At IPO (Aug 2021), promoters held a higher stake. Progressive dilution raises questions: are promoters building a business or exiting? At 27.1%, institutional selling pressure or any large block deal could severely impact the stock. Compare to Bondada Engineering (45.1% and buying more), Borana Weaves (65%), Vintage Coffee — all have strong promoter alignment. Krsnaa at 27.1% is the weakest promoter commitment in the entire analyzed universe.
2
NCD ₹430 Cr — significant debt on a ₹80 Cr PAT business.
₹430 Cr NCD at 7-year tenure. Interest rate unknown but for a mid-cap healthcare company with government receivable concentration, likely 11–13%. At 12% interest: annual interest = ₹51.6 Cr. FY26 PAT = ~₹80 Cr. Net PAT after NCD interest = ~₹28–30 Cr. This is a near-complete wipeout of current PAT. Management justifies this as bridging government receivables — but it also significantly increases financial risk. The leverage ratio will spike from comfortable to concerning.
3
ROE only 8.57% (3-year average) — capital is not being used efficiently.
A healthcare company deploying significant capital into government hospitals and generating only 8.57% ROE is a serious concern. The cause: large capital invested in setting up centers (CT scanners, infrastructure) that take 2–3 years to generate adequate returns. Until the operating leverage thesis plays out, ROE stays low. If FY27 delivers PAT ₹130 Cr on ~₹500 Cr equity, ROE improves to 26% — the single biggest catalyst for rerating. But until then, the ROE is genuinely weak.
4
Government receivable dependency — 152-day debtor days is structural.
Government hospital systems have notoriously slow payment processes. Debtor days of 152 (and rising before Q3 FY26 collection drive) is a structural feature of the PPP model, not a temporary anomaly. The Q3 FY26 collection of ₹130 Cr shows management capability — but also the scale of the problem. With FY27 revenue of ₹900+ Cr, 152 days debtors = ~₹375 Cr permanently locked in receivables. This working capital requirement is why the NCD was needed — but the NCD interest reduces PAT permanently.

India diagnostics sector — where Krsnaa fits
India diagnostics is a ₹14–18B (₹1.2–1.5 lakh Cr) market growing at 12–18% CAGR. The sector has two distinct models: (1) Private B2C labs (Dr Lal, Metropolis, SRL) serving paying patients through their own collection network, and (2) Government PPP (primarily Krsnaa) serving subsidised patients in government hospitals. Krsnaa dominates the PPP segment — but the private B2C segment trades at much higher multiples because margins are higher and working capital is better.
Dr Lal PathLabs — India's largest private pathology network
₹14,000+ Cr mcap · 700+ labs · High margin (20%+ PAT) · Premium B2C brand
Premium private benchmark
Dr Lal is the gold standard of India diagnostics — premium brand, high margin (20%+ PAT), no government receivable risk, excellent working capital. At ₹14,000+ Cr mcap vs Krsnaa's ₹1,900 Cr, Dr Lal is 7x larger. Dr Lal's PAT margin (20%) vs Krsnaa's (10–12%) explains the valuation gap. If Krsnaa successfully grows retail to 30%+ of revenue and improves PAT margins to 15%+, a rerating toward Dr Lal's multiples would be transformational.
Metropolis Healthcare — premium B2C diagnostics
₹8,000+ Cr mcap · 200+ labs · 18%+ PAT margins · Strong urban brand
B2C focus — different model
Metropolis operates premium urban-focused diagnostics — lower volume than Dr Lal but higher ASP tests (genetics, specialised panels). Attractive margins but limited Tier 2/3 reach. Krsnaa's Tier 2/3 PPP network is exactly where Metropolis is absent — the two are complementary, not competitive.
Vijaya Diagnostic Centre — South India PPP-adjacent
₹4,500+ Cr mcap · South India focus · PPP + private hybrid · Better margins than Krsnaa
Partial comparable
Vijaya has both PPP and private revenue (B2C/B2B). Better PAT margins than Krsnaa because it has more private (higher-margin) revenue. At ₹4,500 Cr mcap vs ₹1,900 Cr for Krsnaa — trades at a significant premium. If Krsnaa's retail grows to 25–30% of revenue (improving margin quality), the discount to Vijaya should narrow, giving PE rerating.
